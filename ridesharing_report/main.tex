\begin{abstract}
    We analyse ridesourcing usage intensity (TUI) across Chicago's Community Areas using TNP trip data (2018--2024) and compare it with composite vulnerability indices. A baseline Health \& Socioeconomic Vulnerability Index (HSVI) combines the Hardship Index with the CCVI; the Socio-Demographic Vulnerability Index (SDVI) replaces CCVI with income disadvantage. At the citywide scale, Pearson correlations between mean TUI (2024) and vulnerability are negative and statistically significant, indicating higher ridesourcing intensity in less vulnerable areas rather than a simple pattern of substitute mobility in disadvantaged neighbourhoods. Rank-based associations are weaker and mostly non-significant once outliers are attenuated.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

    \rhostart{R}idesourcing platforms have reshaped urban mobility in large U.S. cities. In Chicago, uneven access to public transport and varying socio-demographic conditions raise a central policy question: does ridesourcing act as a mobility equalizer or as a costly substitute where transit is inadequate?

    We address this at the spatial scale of Chicago's 77 Community Areas (CA), comparing TNP usage intensity with composite vulnerability scores and visualising their joint geography.

\section{Data Sources}

    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{TNP trips} (Chicago Data Portal): monthly pickup counts by Community Area, 2018--2024.
        \item \textbf{Population} (CMAP Community Data Snapshots): resident population by CA and year.
        \item \textbf{Community Area boundaries} (Chicago Data Portal).
        \item \textbf{Hardship Index and per capita income} \cite{chicago_hardship}: socioeconomic indicators by neighborhood (2008--2012 vintage).
        \item \textbf{CCVI} \cite{chicago_ccvi}: Chicago COVID-19 Community Vulnerability Index (auxiliary health indicator).
    \end{itemize}

    Community Area 76 (O'Hare Airport) is excluded from socio-demographic modelling as a transport hub outlier. After merging TNP, population, and vulnerability data, $n=75$ CAs enter the correlation analysis.

\section{Methods}

    \subsection{Transport Usage Index (TUI)}

        TUI measures ridesourcing intensity per resident in Community Area $i$ during period $m$:
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:tui}
            TUI_{i,m} = \frac{\text{TNP pickups}_{i,m}}{\text{population}_{i,m}} \times 1000
        \end{equation}
        We map and correlate the annual mean TUI for 2024. TUI captures usage intensity at the place of trip origin; it should not be equated with individual mobility dependency without controls for public transport access and trip purpose.

    \subsection{Vulnerability indices}

        All composite indices use within-city z-scores. The baseline \textbf{HSVI} combines socioeconomic hardship with health vulnerability:
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:hsvi}
            HSVI_i = \frac{z(\text{Hardship}_i) + z(\text{CCVI}_i)}{2}
        \end{equation}
        The \textbf{SDVI} substitutes income disadvantage for CCVI:
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:sdvi}
            SDVI_i = \frac{z(\text{Hardship}_i) + z(-\text{Income}_i)}{2}
        \end{equation}
        where $\text{Income}_i$ is per capita income. Higher values indicate greater vulnerability. Comparing HSVI and SDVI isolates how replacing a health-oriented component with an explicit income dimension changes the association with TUI.

    \subsection{Correlation analysis}

        We compute Pearson (linear) and Spearman (rank-based) correlations between mean TUI and HSVI, SDVI, and CCVI. Significance is assessed at $\alpha = 0.05$. Results are exploratory and ecological: they describe area-level associations, not individual behaviour.

\section{Results}

    \subsection{Spatial patterns}

        \figref{fig:tui2024} shows a sharp concentration of TUI in central and north-lakefront CAs (Loop, Near North Side, Near West Side), where commercial activity, tourism, and employment destinations generate many pickups relative to resident population. Peripheral and South Side areas generally exhibit lower values.

        \figref{fig:maps} contrasts TUI with SDVI. As the choropleth map shows, there is a stark and undeniable spatial dichotomy between the two indicators, with a clear inverse correlation. The absolute volume of Transportation Network Provider (TNP) usage, represented by the TUI, is heavily concentrated in the central business district (The Loop) and the North Side. Conversely, the highest levels of socio-demographic vulnerability (SDVI) are tightly clustered in the South Side and West Side.
        To be completely candid and objective, these maps mathematically falsify the initial assumption that vulnerable, peripheral neighborhoods utilize ridesharing networks more frequently than affluent ones. The raw data clearly proves that ridesourcing is a premium service predominantly utilized by higher-income and highly educated populations located in the urban core and northern districts. Therefore, continuing to measure raw trip volumes (TUI) will consistently highlight affluent areas, completely missing the underlying socioeconomic distress.
        This finding does not invalidate the project; rather, it requires a more sophisticated analytical pivot to uncover the true sociological issue. To accurately measure spatial inequity, your analysis must shift from "absolute usage" to "financial burden" and "forced dependency."

    \subsection{Bivariate associations}

        \tabref{tab:corr} reports correlation results. Pearson coefficients are negative and significant for all three vulnerability measures: higher TUI tends to co-occur with \emph{lower} composite vulnerability at the CA level. The association is strongest for SDVI ($r=-0.54$), suggesting that adding income to the hardship component slightly tightens the linear fit compared with HSVI ($r=-0.47$).

        Spearman coefficients are weaker. Only CCVI reaches conventional significance ($\rho=-0.26$, $p=0.027$); HSVI and SDVI do not ($p>0.10$). This gap between Pearson and Spearman indicates that a few high-TUI central areas influence the linear slope; the rank-order relationship across the bulk of neighbourhoods is less clear.

\section{Discussion}

    Taken together, the maps and correlations do not support a simple \textit{substitute mobility} narrative at the Community Area scale: areas with higher socio-demographic vulnerability do not show systematically higher ridesourcing intensity per resident. Instead, TUI appears driven largely by central commercial and entertainment geographies, where many trips are generated by workers, visitors, and nightlife rather than by resident populations alone.

    Replacing CCVI with income in the composite index (HSVI $\rightarrow$ SDVI) strengthens the negative linear association slightly but does not reverse its sign. The shift is modest and should not be over-interpreted: hardship and income are correlated, and both socioeconomic indicators post-date the 2024 TNP flows.

    Three limitations bound this interim analysis. First, TUI normalises by resident population but not by daytime population or employment, which overstates intensity in job-rich cores. Second, hardship and income data are not contemporaneous with 2024 trips. Third, public transport accessibility (PTA), the planned third indicator, is not yet integrated; without it, TUI cannot be distinguished from compensatory ridesourcing in transit-poor areas.

    Next steps should add PTA, test spatial autoregressive models to account for neighbourhood contiguity, and compare 2018 vs 2024 TUI to assess temporal change alongside static vulnerability surfaces.
