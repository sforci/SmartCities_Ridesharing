\begin{abstract}
    We analyse ridesourcing across Chicago's Community Areas using TNP data (2018--2024) and composite vulnerability indices. Raw usage intensity (TUI) is highest in the central and North Side cores and correlates negatively with socio-demographic vulnerability. Income-normalised financial burden (Weighted TUI) inverts this pattern on the South and West Sides. Mean ridesharing travel time to the Loop rises toward the urban periphery (7--43 minutes), compounding fare exposure where incomes are lowest. Together, the maps are consistent with spatial mismatch and transport disadvantage rather than ridesourcing as a social equaliser.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

    \rhostart{C}hicago's Functional Urban Area (FUA) concentrates employment and capital in the central business district while many residents live in peripheral communities with weaker transit connectivity. Ridesourcing platforms promise on-demand mobility, but their cost and geography may reinforce rather than reduce spatial inequality.

    We analyse 77 Community Areas (CA), comparing TNP usage intensity, income-normalised financial burden, travel time to the Loop, and composite vulnerability scores.

\section{Data Sources}

    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{TNP trips} (Chicago Data Portal): monthly pickup counts, 2018--2024; trip-level records for travel-time analysis \cite{chicago_tnp}.
        \item \textbf{Population} (CMAP Community Data Snapshots): resident population by CA and year.
        \item \textbf{Community Area boundaries} (Chicago Data Portal) \cite{chicago_boundaries}.
        \item \textbf{Hardship Index and per capita income} \cite{chicago_hardship} (2008--2012 vintage).
        \item \textbf{CCVI} \cite{chicago_ccvi}: auxiliary health vulnerability indicator.
    \end{itemize}

    Community Area 76 (O'Hare Airport) is excluded as a transport-hub outlier ($n=75$ in correlation analyses).

\section{Methods}

    \subsection{Transport Usage Index (TUI)}

        \begin{equation}\label{eq:tui}
            TUI_{i,m} = \frac{\text{TNP pickups}_{i,m}}{\text{population}_{i,m}} \times 1000
        \end{equation}
        Annual mean TUI for 2024 measures ridesourcing intensity per resident at the trip origin.

    \subsection{Vulnerability indices}

        \textbf{SDVI} combines hardship and income disadvantage: $SDVI_i = \text{mean}(z(\text{Hardship}_i), z(-\text{Income}_i))$. Higher values indicate greater socio-demographic vulnerability. \textbf{HSVI} replaces income with CCVI as a health-oriented baseline.

    \subsection{Weighted TUI (Financial Burden Index)}

        With a citywide mean trip cost of \$17.50 (aggregated counts lack fares):
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:wtui}
            \text{Weighted\_TUI}_i = \frac{(\text{n\_trips}_{i,y} \times 17.50) / \text{population}_{i,y}}{\text{Income}_i}
        \end{equation}
        Higher values indicate ridesourcing expenditure as a larger share of per capita income.

    \subsection{Travel time to the Loop}

        From trip-level TNP records (dropoff CA~32), we compute the mean \texttt{trip\_seconds} by pickup CA for 2024. This measures average ridesourcing duration from each origin to the Loop, the city's primary employment core. O'Hare (CA~76) is excluded from origins.

    \subsection{Correlation analysis}

        Pearson and Spearman correlations at $\alpha = 0.05$; ecological associations only.

    \subsection{Financial burden (Weighted TUI)}

        Income normalisation reverses the spatial logic of TUI (\figref{fig:wtui}). The North lakefront shifts to pale tones despite high trip counts: ridesourcing absorbs a small income share. South and West Side CAs (Fuller Park, North Lawndale, Englewood, Riverdale) turn dark red: fewer trips, but a disproportionate budget share.

        \begin{figure}[H]
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{weighted_tui_2024.png}
            \caption{Weighted TUI (Financial Burden Index), 2024.}
            \label{fig:wtui}
        \end{figure}

        Weighted TUI correlates positively with Hardship ($r=0.41$, $p<0.001$) and SDVI ($r=0.29$, $p=0.012$). CAs with Hardship $\geq 60$ show mean burden $1.6\times$ that of CAs with Hardship $<30$.

    \subsection{Travel time to the Loop}

        \figref{fig:looptime} reveals a concentric gradient: mean ridesharing time to CA~32 is shortest at the core (Loop ${\sim}7$\,min, Near North Side ${\sim}10$\,min) and rises toward the periphery. The longest origins reach ${\sim}35$--$43$\,min (far Northwest, e.g.\ Edison Park and Norwood Park). High-hardship South Side communities also face extended trips (Riverdale ${\sim}34$\,min, Chicago Lawn ${\sim}32$\,min, South Chicago ${\sim}29$\,min), though hardship and travel time are not linearly correlated citywide ($r=-0.05$, $p=0.68$).

        \begin{figure}[H]
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{loop_travel_time_2024.png}
            \caption{Mean ridesharing travel time to the Loop by pickup CA, 2024 (minutes).}
            \label{fig:looptime}
        \end{figure}

        The penalty is joint, not unidimensional. Affluent far-Northwest areas endure the longest rides but low Weighted TUI ($r=-0.61$ between travel time and burden, $p<0.001$). Vulnerable South and West Side residents face both above-average travel times and the highest income-normalised spending: longer distances imply higher fares under time-and-distance pricing, on top of already constrained incomes.

\section{Discussion}

    Read together, four visual layers form a coherent account of spatial inequity in Chicago ridesourcing.

    \textbf{The usage paradox.} TUI and SDVI maps (\figref{fig:maps}) show inverse geographies: premium mobility concentrates where vulnerability is lowest. Uber and Lyft are not widely used in absolute terms in the poorest peripheries.

    \textbf{Financial burden.} Weighted TUI (\figref{fig:wtui}) recovers the equity dimension. When residents in disadvantaged areas do use ridesourcing, it consumes a larger income share, consistent with a regressive mobility cost at the ecological scale.

    \textbf{Spatial isolation.} Travel-time maps (\figref{fig:looptime}) document distance from the Loop employment core. Peripheral origins, including much of the South Side, face 25--35\,minute rides under typical traffic conditions, while the core remains within minutes. This pattern is compatible with spatial mismatch theory: residential segregation from job centres raises commuting friction.

    \textbf{The distance--time tariff.} Combining longer rides with higher Weighted TUI on the South and West Sides suggests a compounded penalty. Where radial public transit is slow or unreliable, residents who turn to ridesourcing pay both in duration and in fare. Private smart mobility therefore amplifies, rather than corrects, existing spatial divides. It does not operate as a social equaliser.

    Within the broader FUA, Chicago's scale and commuter flows intensify competition for central opportunities; South Side residents enter that market from a dual disadvantage of longer ridesharing times and tighter income constraints. Full validation would require integrating public transport accessibility (PTA), the planned third indicator, and household-level expenditure data. Socioeconomic indicators also pre-date 2024 trips. Even so, the evidence supports interpreting peripheral vulnerability as a form of transport disadvantage, not as high ridesourcing dependency.
